Data Set

The data set that we used gives advanced nutrition facts about the composition of various different foods and cooking methods. The data set is quite vast, so the main food groups have been separated into enumerated groups based on traits.

Initial Analysis

We decided to use bar chart visualizations featuring all of the nutrients. At first we thought a bubble chart would be a nice interaction, however it did not make the impact a simple bar chart did.


We are using a data set about nutrients present in different foods and would like to simplify the data by subcoding a food group to each food item to make the graphs easier to interpret and understand. The bar chart is meant to show overall average content by food group. This way you can easily examine nutritional content across 4 main food groupings : Fruits and Vegetables, Meats and Eggs, Dairy and Grains, and finally, Fats and Prepared Foods.


The questions we wanted to answer had to do with what we might think naturally would be in the particular food groups. Our hypothesis was that the Meat and Eggs group would have a lot of protein content, Fats and Prepared Foods, a lot of sodium, Dairy and Grains, calcium, and finally, Fruits and Vegetables would contain the most Vitamin C.


These did prove true in each case. Moreover, fiber was the highest content of Dairy and Grains and water was the highest in Fruits and Vegetables, which also makes sense.


Finally, when looking at the Fats and Prepared Foods, the highest nutrients were sodium and sugars.

Our analysis did ring true with our natural thoughts of these nutrients. We were successful in visually representing the comparisons of these essential (and some not so essential) nutrients.

Procedures

  • Group data by food group and subcoding
  • Separate and parse data as .csv
  • Link .csv data with d3 in html file
  • Create the bar charts and link interactions
  • Sort by nutrient content and food groups

The color palette was determined in the Color Brewer. I used the hex colors that most represented the chosen nutrient. For example, for potassium, we used a bright yellow (think b-a-n-a-n-a-s!). For water, we used blue and Vitamin C, orange etc.
